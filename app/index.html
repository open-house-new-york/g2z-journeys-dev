Collection

Every day, more than 6,000 men and women of the Department of Sanitation collect garbage from residences and public buildings in New York City.

 

They work in pairs and collect more than 10,000 tons of material every day. All that waste goes into the back of compactor trucks.

 

When trucks are full, they head to one of the cityâ€™s transfer stations.

Transfer

Transfer stations are consolidation centers for waste. They combine material from several collection trucks into containers to make long-distance travel cheaper and more efficient.

Tipping

Inside the transfer station, trucks unload their content onto a lower level known as the "tipping floor".

Compaction

Front loaders place waste on treadmills, which drop it into compactors.

Containerization

After being compacted, waste is loaded into containers for long-distance transport.

Export

Virtually no waste generated in NYC stays within the city.

Waste is then transported on trains or trailer trucks to landfills outside of NYC.

It will travel to other cities and states, located from 70 up to 600 miles away.

Disposal

Landfills are essentially man-made mountains of waste, covered with soil.

They follow environmental regulations to prevent water and soil contamination.

Compacting

Heavy machinery will move, compact, and cover the waste with soil.

This process continues until several layers of waste have been put in place.

Piling up

Landfills are essentially man-made mountains of waste.

They can reach hundreds of feet in height.
