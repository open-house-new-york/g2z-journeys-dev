From ocean dumping, to building land, to building mountains, to sending it elsewhere.

What we do with our waste in New York City has changed dramatically over the last 120 years.

1880's

When streets were filthy

In the late 19th century, NYC's streets were incredibly dirty. Food scraps, horse manure, ashes, and other types of waste were common obstacles to pedestrians and carts on the streets.

1885

Colonel Waring and the "White Wigs"

The modern story of New York's waste starts with the creation of the Department of Street Cleaning (DSC) in 1881.

During its first years, the DSC was unable to offer a solution to the pressing issue of street cleaning.

It was only in 1885 when the reformed Civil War Colonel George Waring took over the DSC that things began to change.

Sweeping and collection

Waring first challenge was to ensure that the DSC was actually cleaning the streets and collecting garbage.

To do this, Waring reorganized DSC's labor force and its practices.

He issued white uniforms to sweepers (which became known as the "white wings") and had them work alongside drivers, loading the garbage into horse pulled carts.

Transfering waste to boats

Carts loaded with waste were driven to wharves on the waterfront. There, they were tipped onto large, flat bottomed boats, called scows.

Loading the scows

Men, known as "scow trimmers," manually shoveled waste on the boats, so that the load would be evenly distributed.

In this video from 1903, we can see carts dumping waste on scows, and the scow trimmers in action.

Disposal: ocean dumping

When Colonel Waring took over the DSC in 1885, most of its waste was dumped from scows into the Atlantic Ocean.

While Waring recognized that ocean dumping was a poor practice, it would take decades before DSC was able to fully move away from it.

Dumping off Sandy Hook

Tugboats pulled the scows loaded with waste to the Atlantic Ocean, right outside the Lower New York Bay.

There, scows were emptied manually. Some of the debris floated and eventually ended back in beaches and rivers.

Looking for alternatives

To move away from ocean dumping, Waring specified that waste should be separated waste into three categories which could use other diposal methods.

Organic material was turned into fertilizer at plants outside of the city; paper and rags were recovered for their market value; and the resulting rubbish was either incinerated or landfilled along with ash.

Disposal: building land on waste

As an alternative to ocean dumping, waste was used as landfill to expand shorelines or to create islands. This was a common practice in NYC until the mid 20th century.

This method, often called "land reclamation," had a double benefit: it was seen as more sanitary than ocean dumping, and created land which could be used or sold.

Much of NYC is built on waste

As a result of land reclamation practices, many parts of the NYC that we know today are built on waste.

1934

Ending ocean dumping

Ocean dumping was only banned in 1934, as the result from a court order, updheld by the Supreme Court. In the following decades, New York utilized several incinerators and landfills for the disposal of its waste.

1947

The creation of Fresh Kills landfill

In 1947, the city created a landfill on Staten Island masrhland known as Fresh Kills.

Initially meant to be a temporary solution, Fresh Kills would eventually outlive all other landfills and incinerators in the city.

1950's

Separation of private waste

In the 1950's, the City stopped collecting waste from businesses, and continued to collect only from residents and public institutions.

This split in NYC's waste system would greatly influence how the city deals with waste today.

1960's-1970's

Increased environmental regulations

In the 1960's and 70's, the federal government enacted a series of environmental regulations regarding solid waste.

Acts from 1965 and 1970 regulated sanitary landfills and led to the closure of several open dumps.

The Clean Air Act of 1970's led to incinerator closures because of stricter emissions standards.

1980's-1990's

Growing dependence on Fresh Kills

With the closure of other disposal sites in NYC, Fresh Kills landfill on Saten Island increasingly became the only in-city disposal option.

Fresh Kills occupied 2,200 acres of land and at its peak received about 13,000 tons of waste per day.

1990's

A fundamental split

With Fresh Kills landfill almost full in the 1990's, the city decided to raise costs for private companies to dipose of their waste there.

These private companies reacted by creating a network of in-city "transfer stations." Inside transfer stations, waste was placed on larger trucks and sent it to cheaper landfills in other cities and states.

2001

The closure of Fresh Kills

In 1996, the City pleged to close Fresh Kills landfill in five years. It was then estimated that if the landfill was kept open, it would have become the highest point on the eastern seaboard.

After Fresh Kills was finally closed in 2001, the City began using the private transfer stations to send all of its waste outside of NYC.

2017

From landfill to park

Since its closure, Fresh Kills has been slated to become a park, approximately three times the size of Central Park.

Designed by the landscape architecture firm James Corner Field Operations, the park is expected to be completed in 2037.

But this is just a part of the story.

Today, the City is trying to deal with the legacy of its waste history. Specifically, it has pledged to to eliminate waste sent to landfills by the year 2030.
