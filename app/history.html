From ocean dumping, to building land, to building mountains, to sending it elsewhere.

What we do with our waste in New York City has changed dramatically over the last 120 years.

1880's

When streets were filthy

In the late 19th century, NYC's streets were incredibly dirty. Food scraps, horse manure, ashes, and other types of waste were common obstacles to pedestrians and carts on the streets.

1885

Colonel Waring and the "White Wings"

The story of New York's modern waste system starts with the creation of the Department of Street Cleaning (DSC) in 1881.

During its first years, the DSC was unable to offer a solution to the pressing issue of street cleaning. Several factors hindered its efficiency, including shared the responsability of cleaning the streets with several other City agencies, and the lack of oversight of its contracts.

It was only in 1885 when a Civil War Colonel named George Waring took over the DSC that things began to change.

Sweeping and collection

Waring's first challenge was to ensure that the DSC was actually cleaning the streets and collecting garbage. To do this, he reorganized DSC's labor force and its practices.

Waring instituted a military-like hierarchy, with a clear chain of command and responsabilities for the cleaning of the city's newly defined sanitation sections.

Waring also issued white uniforms to sweepers (who became known as the "white wings") and had them work alongside drivers, loading the garbage into horse-drawn carts.

Transfering waste to boats

Carts loaded with waste were driven to wharves on the waterfront. There, they were tipped onto large, flat bottomed boats, called scows.

Loading the scows

Men, known as "scow trimmers," manually shoveled waste on the boats, so that the load would be evenly distributed.

In this video from 1903, we can see carts dumping waste on scows, and the scow trimmers in action.

Disposal: ocean dumping

In the very early days of New York City, waste was disposed of in the Hudson and East Rivers, sometimes being used to expand the shoreline.

When Colonel Waring took over the DSC in 1885, NYC's waste production had grown so much that almost all of its waste was dumped into the Atlantic Ocean.

Dumping off Sandy Hook

Tugboats pulled the scows loaded with waste to the Atlantic Ocean, right outside the Lower New York Bay.

There, scows were emptied manually. Some of the debris floated and eventually ended back in beaches and rivers.

Looking for alternatives

To move away from ocean dumping, Waring specified that waste should be separated into three categories that could be disposed of using other methods, even creating one of the city’s first (if short-lived) municipal recycling programs.

Organic material was turned into fertilizer at plants outside of the city; paper and rags were recovered for their market value; and the resulting rubbish was either incinerated or landfilled along with ash.

Disposal: building land on waste

As an alternative to ocean dumping, waste was used as landfill to expand shorelines or to create islands. This was a common practice in NYC until the mid 20th century.

This method, often called "land reclamation," had a double benefit: it was seen as more sanitary than ocean dumping, and created land which could be used or sold.

Much of NYC is built on waste

As a result of land reclamation practices, many parts of the NYC that we know today are built on waste. *Daniel C. Walsh and Robert G. LaFleur, via Darcy Bender.

1934

Ending ocean dumping

In spite of Waring's early attempts, ocean dumping of municipal waste was only banned in 1934.

The ban was the result from a court order, which was obtained by New Jersey residents to stop New York from dumping waste in the Atlantic Ocen. This order was updheld by the Supreme Court, making the ban federal.

1930's-1970's

A network of landfills and incinerators

In the decades following the ban of ocean dumping, New York utilized several incinerators and landfills for the disposal of its waste.

Right after the ban, New York had a total of 89 active landfills and 22 incinerators. *DSNY Annual Report, 2005.

1947

The creation of Fresh Kills landfill

In 1947, the city created a landfill on Staten Island marshland known as Fresh Kills.

Initially meant to be a temporary solution, Fresh Kills would eventually outlive all other landfills and incinerators in the city.

1950's

Separation of private waste

In the 1950's, the City stopped collecting waste from businesses, and continued to collect only from residents and public institutions.

This split in NYC's waste system would greatly influence how the city deals with waste in decades to come.

1960's-1970's

Increased environmental regulations

In the 1960's and 70's, the federal government enacted a series of environmental regulations regarding solid waste.

The Clean Air Act of 1970 led to incinerator closures because of stricter emissions standards.

Acts from 1965 and 1970 regulated sanitary landfills and led to the closure of several open dumps, such as this one in New Jersey.

1980's-1990's

Growing dependence on Fresh Kills

With the closure of incinerators and smaller ladfills in NYC because of environmental regulations, Fresh Kills landfill on Saten Island increasingly became the only in-city disposal option.

Despite presenting problems under the new environmental regulations, it was allowed to remain open while being retrofitted through a Consent Order between the City and the State.

 

At the peak of its use in 1986-87, Fresh Kills received up to 29,000 tons of waste per day. Today, it occupies 2,200 acres and holds about 150 million tons of waste. *Fresh Kills Park Site History.

1990's

A fundamental split

With Fresh Kills landfill almost full in the 1990's, the city decided to raise costs for private companies to dipose of their waste there.

These private companies reacted by creating a network of in-city "transfer stations." Inside transfer stations, waste was placed on larger trucks and then sent to cheaper landfills in other cities and states.

Clustered transfer stations

These private transfer stations were built on land zoned for industrial use, many times near poor communities and communities of color.

To this day, despite efforts from the City to pursue a more equitable distribution, most of the private transfer stations are still clustered in a few areas of NYC. *U.S. Census Bureau, 2006-2010 ACS;
NYSDEC Transfer Station Annual Reports, 2015.

2001

The closure of Fresh Kills

In 1996, the City pleged to close Fresh Kills landfill in five years. It was then estimated that if the landfill was kept open, it would have become the highest point on the eastern seaboard.

After Fresh Kills was finally closed in 2001, the City began using private transfer stations to send all of its waste outside of NYC. This meant increased reliance on trucks for waste export, leading to more truck traffic and air polution.

2017

From landfill to park

Since its closure, Fresh Kills has been slated to become a park, which will be approximately three times the size of Central Park.

Designed by the landscape architecture firm James Corner Field Operations, the park is expected to be completed in 2037.

Transforming the waste system

Today, the City is trying to deal with the legacy of its waste history. It has built new transfer stations, increasing the use of rail and barge transportation for waste disposal. These initiatives seek to reduce air polution and transfer station concentration.

But this is just a part of the story.

In 2015, the City pledged to to eliminate all waste sent to landfills by the year 2030. This will require increasing alernatives to landfilling, such as recycling and composting.
